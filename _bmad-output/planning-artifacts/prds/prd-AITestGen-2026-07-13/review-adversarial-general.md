# Adversarial Review — Application Intelligence Platform V1 PRD

**Reviewer stance:** skeptical staff engineer / competitor analyst. Findings below deliberately exclude the four issues already flagged in the PRD itself (non-prod not technically enforced; confidence/risk scoring cut; direct-commit overwrite risk; SSO/MFA handoff mechanism undefined) — those are known. Everything below is either an internal inconsistency, an untestable requirement, a glossed-over hard problem, a legal/business exposure, or a Vision/Success-Metric claim the FRs don't actually back up.

---

## 1. "Trusted Knowledge Model" is built with a 4-verb toolset with no create and no real edit — the marketing claim outstrips the mechanism

§4.4 calls human review "the trust mechanism at the center of the product," and Vision (§1) says the human can "approve, correct, or reject" discovered journeys. But FR-9 through FR-15 only give the reviewer **approve / reject / rename / delete** — and the Out of Scope note under FR-13 explicitly rules out merging, splitting, or reassigning which pages/actions belong to a Journey. There is also no FR anywhere allowing a reviewer to *manually create* a Journey or Capability the AI missed entirely.

So if the AI mis-groups two workflows into one Journey, or invents a spurious one, or simply never surfaces a real journey, the reviewer's only lever is delete-and-hope, not correct. "Correct" in the Vision statement is doing a lot of unearned work — the actual toolset is closer to a moderation queue than an editorial one. This directly undercuts SM-2 ("light correction rather than heavy rework") as a meaningful accuracy proxy: the tool doesn't let reviewers *do* rework even if they wanted to, so a low correction rate could just as easily mean the review UI is too blunt to express disagreement, not that the AI is accurate.

## 2. FR-24 "passing Test Assets" promises data the platform has no mechanism to obtain

FR-24 states coverage analytics shows which Journeys are backed by "generated/**passing**" Test Assets. But FR-21 explicitly states pipeline wiring is manual/template-based — the customer, not the platform, wires tests into their CI run step — and there is no FR anywhere describing the platform polling CI, receiving a webhook, or ingesting a test-results artifact. §13 Integration and Dependencies lists only the export direction (repo/pipeline targets) with no read-back channel.

This is a direct contradiction between FR-21 and FR-24: one says the platform doesn't touch the customer's pipeline, the other assumes it somehow knows pass/fail state from that same pipeline. As written, FR-24 is unimplementable without an undocumented dependency (a results-ingestion API/webhook) that appears nowhere else in the document, including the Integration section that exists specifically to enumerate this kind of dependency.

## 3. FR-6's "exercises UI actions and forms" has no destructive-action guardrail — legal/operational exposure exists even in a fully compliant non-production environment

The task brief already flags that V1 doesn't technically block production targeting. Separately and more concretely: even a *correctly* designated non-production environment routinely has real-world side effects wired into it — staging databases cloned from production with real customer PII, shared third-party sandbox accounts that still send real emails/SMS (Twilio/SendGrid test tiers frequently deliver for real), payment gateway "test mode" integrations that still notify finance/fraud teams, or "Delete Account," "Cancel Subscription," and "Send to All Customers" actions that are destructive regardless of environment tier.

FR-6 says the platform "autonomously... exercises UI actions and forms, and invokes APIs" with zero mention of an exclusion list, confirmation-dialog avoidance, or any judgment about irreversible/high-blast-radius actions. A "thorough tester" (the Vision's own framing) knows not to click "Permanently Delete" or "Send Password Reset to Real Email" during exploratory testing; nothing in the FRs gives the AI that judgment. This is a real legal/operational exposure — spamming real inboxes, destroying seeded test data other teams depend on, or tripping a customer's own fraud/anomaly detection — that exists independent of the already-flagged production-targeting risk, and it is not mentioned anywhere in §11 Constraints and Guardrails or §12 Risk Register.

## 4. No guardrail against the crawler being treated as an attacker by the target application's own defenses

Related to #3 but distinct: autonomous, exhaustive form/API exercising at machine speed is exactly the behavior WAFs, rate limiters, and account-lockout policies exist to catch. FR-5's time budget protects the *platform* from runaway exploration; nothing protects the *target application* from being rate-limited, IP-banned, or having its Dedicated Test Account locked out mid-run — which in shared-infrastructure staging environments (common in enterprises that don't fully isolate non-prod) can cascade into throttling other teams' legitimate traffic. This operational risk isn't mentioned in §10 Reliability NFRs (which only cover the platform's own time-budget completion) or §12 Risk Register.

## 5. Vision's "without anyone having had to remember to document it" is false as scoped — someone has to remember to re-run discovery

Vision (§1) sells the product on eliminating the burden of remembering to document what the app does. But per the Addendum, "discovery is a point-in-time crawl, not continuous observation," and FR-15 only flags new Journeys on a **manually re-triggered** discovery run — there's no scheduled/automatic re-discovery FR. So the map goes silently stale the moment nobody remembers to re-run it, which is the exact failure mode the Vision claims to solve. This is a Vision claim the FR set doesn't support, separate from the already-flagged drift-detection non-goal (that's about detecting change *within* a journey; this is about the much more basic problem of nobody re-running discovery at all).

## 6. FR-15's "flag only newly-discovered Journeys" quietly requires the same journey-matching capability the PRD disclaims as out of scope

FR-15 requires the platform to distinguish "newly-discovered" Journeys from ones "not seen in a prior run" across re-discovery runs — which means matching Journey B in run 2 to Journey A in run 1 as "the same journey" (or not). But §4.4's Out of Scope note and §5 Non-Goals both state V1 "cannot detect that a previously-approved Journey's underlying runtime behavior has changed." Cross-run journey identity/matching and cross-run change detection are the same underlying technical problem — if the system can reliably say "this is a new journey we haven't seen before," it necessarily has some basis for comparison that could just as easily flag "this journey's steps changed." The PRD asserts the platform can do one and not the other without reconciling why. As written, FR-15 is either quietly harder than scoped, or it's actually doing coarse change detection under a different name and the Non-Goal is narrower than it sounds.

## 7. FR-16's "negative/edge-case scenario" coverage is unfalsifiable as written

FR-16 requires Scenario generation to cover "both happy-path and negative/edge-case scenarios" with no definition of sufficiency, no enumeration strategy, and no acceptance criterion. There's no way to write a test for this FR — "did it generate enough negative cases" has no bar. Contrast with FR-7's stop conditions, which are crisply testable (exhaustive traversal or time budget). FR-16 reads like an aspiration dressed as a requirement, and because Scenarios aren't human-reviewable before Playwright generation (FR-18), a shallow or nonsensical "negative case" ships straight into the customer's suite unchecked.

## 8. No human ever reviews the actual generated test logic — approval happens one full abstraction layer above what ships

The review gate (FR-9–FR-14) operates at the Journey level; FR-18 explicitly forecloses editing Scenarios before Playwright generation. That means the QA Director's approval is a sign-off on a business-language *label and grouping*, never on the actual assertions, selectors, or test logic that gets committed to the customer's repository (and in direct-commit mode, potentially straight into their branch with zero other human touching it). A team could "trust" a Journey-level approval while the underlying generated Playwright test asserts the wrong thing, uses a brittle selector, or tests a triviality — and nothing in the review workflow would catch it before it's live in CI. This meaningfully weakens the "human-in-the-loop trust mechanism" pitch (§1, §4.4): the human is in the loop for taxonomy, not for the artifact that actually runs.

## 9. FR-20's four CI/CD providers are not remotely equivalent integration efforts, but are presented as one bullet-point FR

GitHub Actions, GitLab CI, and Azure DevOps are each a single hosted API/OAuth surface. Jenkins is not a product — it's a self-hosted, plugin-dependent, wildly heterogeneous ecosystem with no standard "the Jenkins API." "Supports repository/pipeline targets for... Jenkins" is not a testable claim without specifying which Jenkins configuration, plugin set, or auth model is assumed. Bundling it alongside three SaaS-native platforms as if it's the same unit of engineering work is a hand-wave that will surface as a scope surprise during implementation.

## 10. No data-handling NFR for the sensitive application data the crawler will inevitably capture

FR-6 has the platform autonomously capture "pages, navigation paths, actions, forms, API calls, and state transitions" — which, for the PRD's own flagship example (an insurer's claims-processing staging environment, UJ-1), means claims data, potentially PII, and other regulated-adjacent content flowing into the platform's storage. §10 Cross-Cutting NFRs and §11 Privacy only address *credential* handling ("Dedicated Test Account," "secret handling") — there is no NFR for retention, encryption, access control, or deletion of the *captured application content* itself, which for an insurance/healthcare/financial pilot customer is likely the more sensitive dataset. Given multi-application, multi-tenant hosted SaaS is in scope from V1 (FR-25, FR-26), tenant isolation of this captured content isn't addressed either.

## 11. On-prem AI quality parity is assumed, not engineered for

FR-27 requires on-prem deployments to bring their own AI provider API keys/endpoint, with the AI/LLM processing running fully in-customer-network. Nothing in the NFRs or FRs acknowledges that journey-inference quality (the entire value proposition, and the thing SM-2 measures) is a direct function of model quality — so an on-prem customer who points the platform at a weaker or smaller in-network model could get a materially worse product with no FR-level acknowledgment that this variance exists, is measured, or is bounded. "The platform runs the AI in-network" quietly assumes "and it works about as well," which is exactly the kind of hard problem the FR glosses over by omission.

## 12. Glossary states an absolute guarantee that later sections walk back — a framing/rhetoric inconsistency, not just a substance gap

§3 Glossary defines Non-Production Environment with the sentence "V1 **must never** run discovery against a production-facing environment" — phrased as an ironclad system property. A reader stopping at the Glossary (a section whose entire purpose is to state facts precisely) would reasonably conclude this is enforced. The actual mechanism (entirely customer responsibility, no technical check) is disclosed three sections later in §11 and again in §12. This isn't the same finding as the already-known enforcement gap — it's that the document's own glossary asserts the property in absolute, guaranteed-sounding language it doesn't itself believe, which is a credibility problem independent of the underlying risk.

## 13. UJ-2's "release readiness" resolution is not actually supported by what coverage analytics can show

UJ-2 has Devon approve a release "with a documented view of what's covered, rather than a gut call." But coverage analytics (FR-24) can only speak to whether *approved, discovered* Journeys have passing tests — it says nothing about Journeys the discovery run never found (partial map, FR-7), Journeys still sitting unreviewed in the queue, or functionality entirely outside the Discovery Scope (FR-4). A dashboard that's silent about undiscovered/unscoped functionality is not "what's covered" in the sense a release-readiness decision needs — it's "what's covered among what we happened to find," which is a materially weaker and easily-misread claim. Devon's confidence in the journey is a bigger claim than the underlying FRs can actually back.

## 14. FR-25's multi-application executive dashboard is core V1 scope built for a persona explicitly declared secondary

§2.1 states the CTO ("portfolio-level visibility across applications") is secondary and not the primary design target for this release; V1 is "built and sold around the Engineering Leader + QA Director relationship first." Yet FR-25 — a multi-application rollup dashboard — ships as core V1 scope (§6.1), and none of the three Success Metrics (SM-1/2/3) or the two Key User Journeys meaningfully exercise the cross-application rollup use case (UJ-2 mentions "two applications" only in passing, with no multi-app-specific climax or resolution). This is scope built for a persona the PRD itself says isn't the design target, with no success criterion that would tell V1 whether it was worth building.
