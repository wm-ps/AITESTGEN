# PRD Quality Review — Application Intelligence Platform (V1)

## Overall verdict
This PRD is unusually disciplined about honesty: explicit Non-Goals, an honest [NOTE FOR PM] on a scope deviation from the approved brief, a Risk Register that admits unmitigated risk, and Success Metrics that map directly to specific FR ranges rather than measuring vanity activity. Its weaknesses are downstream-facing rather than strategic: most FRs — including the single most important one, AI journey/capability inference (FR-8), and all four analytics views (FR-22–FR-25) — state a capability without a testable consequence, and the Risk Register / Constraints section repeatedly cites Open Question numbers that no longer match the current Open Questions list. Both are fixable without touching the PRD's substance, but both would trip up architecture and story-creation work if carried forward as-is.

## Decision-readiness — strong
Trade-offs are named with what was given up, not smoothed over. §11 states plainly that non-production enforcement "is a customer responsibility, not a platform-enforced constraint" and cross-references the residual risk in §12. §5's Non-Goals carries a real `[NOTE FOR PM]` at a genuine tension — "the approved brief named risk/confidence scoring as a V1 outcome — this is a deliberate scope deviation" — rather than at a safe checkpoint. §12 Risk 3 admits "Mitigation: None built into V1" instead of manufacturing a false mitigation. The eight Open Questions (§8) are genuinely unresolved, not rhetorical (e.g., Q6 on direct-commit overwrite conflict has no answer anywhere else in the document). No findings.

## Substance over theater — strong
The Vision (§1) is specific to this product's mechanism ("Given only a URL and dedicated test credentials... explores a non-production instance"), and explicitly disclaims false differentiation: "V1 does not claim technical discovery superiority." The addendum's Competitive Positioning section goes further, naming three competitors with the same discovery capability — the opposite of innovation theater.

### Findings
- **low** Secondary-persona padding (§2.1) — Four of six JTBD entries (CTO, Product Owner, Architect, Test Teams) don't drive any FR, UJ, or MVP decision in this PRD; the document itself concedes they're "not the primary design target for this release." Not full personas and self-aware about their status, so this is minor, but it edges toward the rubric's "personas that don't drive a single decision" pattern. *Fix:* either cut to one line each without JTBD-quote framing, or note explicitly that they're included for stakeholder-awareness only, not design input.

## Strategic coherence — strong
The thesis is stated once and then actually driven through the document: §7 frames V1 success as "proving the core thesis (runtime discovery → journey mapping → generated tests) holds up in real pilots" rather than a customer-count or revenue target. Each Success Metric is explicitly bound to an FR range (SM-1 → FR-9–FR-25, SM-2 → FR-8–FR-13, SM-3 → FR-16–FR-21), which is a rigor level most PRDs don't reach. A counter-metric (SM-C1) is named against the one metric most gameable by over-splitting Journeys. Feature order in §4 follows the thesis (onboard → discover → infer → review/trust → generate → deliver → make legible), not ease-of-build order. No findings.

## Done-ness clarity — thin
Downstream story creation leans on this dimension hardest, and coverage is uneven. Only 3 of the PRD's 27 FRs (FR-1, FR-3, FR-7) carry an explicit "Consequences (testable)" block; the rest state a capability without a verifiable condition. Two clusters matter most:

### Findings
- **high** No testable definition of "business language" output (§4.3, FR-8) — FR-8 is the core AI behavior the whole product depends on ("transform captured discovery signals into candidate Business Capabilities and Journeys expressed in business language, not raw technical labels"), but nothing in the FR or its Notes states how "business language" vs. "raw technical label" would be judged as done. This is the FR every downstream review-UX and QA-acceptance decision hangs off. *Fix:* add at least one concrete negative example (a route-name label that would fail) and one positive example, or a reviewer-facing check the human-review step (FR-9) is meant to catch.
- **high** Analytics/dashboard FRs are capability statements only (§4.7, FR-22–FR-25) — "Platform provides a Capability Map view," "...a Journey Explorer — a detail view of a Journey's screens, actions, and API calls," "...coverage analytics showing which approved Journeys are backed by..." and the executive dashboard are each one sentence with no fields, filter, or interaction spelled out, and no consequence an engineer could verify against. This is a large fraction of what a customer actually sees (§1 calls these views the thing leadership uses to justify release decisions). *Fix:* add a short "shows at minimum..." consequence per FR, or route to a UX spec explicitly and say so in the FR.
- **medium** Review-workflow FRs (§4.4, FR-9–FR-13) and CI/CD-delivery FRs (§4.6, FR-19–FR-21) are terse CRUD/capability statements without consequences. Several are plausibly self-evident (Approve/Reject/Rename/Delete on a record), which is why this is medium rather than high, but FR-19's "customer configures which mode per Application" and FR-21's "instructions/a template" have no stated shape or acceptance bar. *Fix:* at minimum, note where these are self-evidently testable vs. where a UX/architecture spec must supply the missing detail.

## Scope honesty — adequate
§5 Non-Goals is doing real work, not template filler — eleven explicit exclusions, each with a reason, several with a `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` tag (§4.6 FR-21 Notes) or an inline "Out of Scope" callout at the FR itself (FR-11, FR-15, FR-18, FR-25). This is scope honesty at its best: de-scoping is argued, not silently assumed.

### Findings
- **high** Open Question 8 is a phase-blocker hiding as a routine open item (§8) — "For SSO/MFA-protected apps (FR-3), how does the customer actually capture and hand off the reusable session state... Mechanism isn't yet specified." FR-3's entire non-standard-login path (explicitly called out as a hard onboarding precondition in §2.2) depends on this mechanism, yet it's listed alongside lower-stakes questions (e.g., Q1's "hours not days" claim) without being flagged as blocking. Given §0 states this PRD is meant to hand off to "engineering, design, and QA stakeholders building... the first release," this should be triaged and resolved (or explicitly deferred with an owner) before it reaches architecture. *Fix:* either promote Q8 to a `[NOTE FOR PM]` at FR-3 itself, or resolve it before finalize.

## Downstream usability — thin
Glossary (§3), FR-ID continuity (FR-1 through FR-27, no gaps or duplicates), and UJ protagonist naming (Maria, Devon, both with inline context) are all solid — this PRD is chain-top (feeds engineering/design/QA per §0) and mostly behaves like one. But the Risk Register and Constraints section have a recurring, verifiable cross-reference bug that would actively mislead a reader following the citations.

### Findings
- **high** Open-Question cross-references are systematically off by one (§11, §12) — §11 Constraints ("see §12 Risk Register item 1 and Open Question 4") and §12 Risk 1 ("Unresolved: whether V1 needs a technical safeguard (Open Question 4)") both point to Open Question 4, but the production-safeguard question is actually **Open Question 3** (§8 item 3); Open Question 4 is "What are the hosted SaaS data residency/retention specifics?" — an unrelated topic. The same pattern repeats twice more: §12 Risk 3 cites "(Open Question 6)" for the confidence-scoring revisit question, which is actually **Open Question 5**; §12 Risk 4 cites "(Open Question 7)" for the direct-commit-overwrite question, which is actually **Open Question 6**. All four citations point one item past their intended target, suggesting the Open Questions list was reordered after the Risk Register was written. *Fix:* re-point all four citations (§11 and §12 items 1, 3, 4) to Open Questions 3, 5, and 6 respectively, and re-check every other numeric cross-reference in the document for the same drift.

## Shape fit — strong
This is a multi-stakeholder enterprise B2B product with meaningful review-workflow UX, so UJs with named protagonists are load-bearing — and the PRD delivers exactly two, scoped deliberately to the two personas it says the release is "built and sold around" (§2.1), rather than inflating to one UJ per JTBD entry. That's good shape discipline: neither over-formalized (no UJ padding for the four secondary personas) nor under-formalized (the two personas that matter for V1 design decisions each get a full entry-state/path/climax/resolution/edge-case journey). No findings.

## Mechanical notes
- **Assumptions Index framing is stale relative to the current PRD body.** §0 states "Inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags mark inferences made during drafting that need explicit confirmation; all are indexed in §9," but the current PRD body contains no live `[ASSUMPTION: …]` tags — both §9 entries are a "confirmed decision" note and a `[NOTE FOR PM]` reference, and §9's closing parenthetical confirms the only two prior assumptions were resolved and removed. The roundtrip mechanism described in §0 no longer has anything to point at; either update §0's framing or note explicitly that all assumptions have been resolved.
- **Glossary shorthand drift (low).** The glossary defines "Business Capability" and "Business Journey," but FR text and section headers consistently shorten these to "Capability" / "Journey" (e.g., FR-9, FR-22, §6.1). This reads as intentional shorthand rather than accidental drift given how consistent it is, but it's worth confirming that's the intent since downstream extraction tools may key off the full glossary term.
- FR IDs (FR-1–FR-27), SM IDs (SM-1–SM-3, SM-C1), and UJ IDs (UJ-1, UJ-2) are all contiguous and unique — no gaps or duplicates found.
